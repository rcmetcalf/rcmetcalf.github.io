     XML, HTML, and CSS all have specific standards of use on the web. As Dr. Berquist said, it is helpful to know the web standards "so that we can know when and why we might have to deviate from them." Although I had worked with databases before, I did not now about the different types of relationships between entities. Here's what I learned:

* A one-to-one relationship is a connection where a first table is only associated to a second table at one point, and that second table is only associated to the first table at one point. Ex: A wife only has one husband, and a husband only has one wife. (This relationships is not common in databases)
* A one-to-many relationship is a connection where a first table is asscoiated to many points in a second table, but the second table is only associated to one point in the first table. Ex: A man has many sons, but the son only has one father. (This relationship is the most common)
* A many-to-many relationship is a connection where a first table is associated to many points in a second table, and the second table is associated to many points in the first table. Ex: A family can have many pets, and a pet can have many owners (members of the family). (This relationship is less common than one-to-many, but it can be essential in the database)

3. Is understanding of the structures important for citizens of our information environment, or can an individual succeed without understanding how things work?
Share your discoveries.

     Before I learned about information structures, I rarely thought about the time and resources put into web and other electronic management. I could navigate the web just fine, even though I didn't know about HTML or CSS. But now that I know about these structures, I think that it is very important for all people to know at least the basics of information structures.

     More and more aspects of daily life are being incorporated into the web. Schools place course information online, people use Google religiously, and a personal webpage can be used as a resume. If people learn how to code for themselves, they will be able to stay up-to-date with changes in information technology, or even contribute to it themselves. I know that I personally view the Internet and other information systems differently now that I know how they are organized and built. For example, when I receive "404 errors" now, I understand that the webpage could be missing because of a incorrectly named link path. Before I knew about information structures, I thought that error was caused by dropped wifi or a faulty computer.

     Understanding information structures may not be necessary to use the web or make a basic table, but it definitely benefits people to know why and how these concpets work. You don't have to know how your car's engine works to drive it, but it's very useful to understand how it works in case your car breaks down. Same thing goes for information structures. Knowing how codes and databases work can be very helpful when you have to troubleshoot technology problems.
